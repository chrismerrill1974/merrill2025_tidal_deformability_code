\begin{abstract}
We compute the tidal deformability of neutron stars within Recursive Dimensionality Theory (RDT), a framework in which effective spatial dimensionality increases logarithmically with matter density above nuclear saturation. Building on Paper III, which demonstrated that RDT-modified Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff equations produce neutron stars with $\sim$2\% larger radii and $\sim$5\% higher maximum masses, we solve the relativistic tidal perturbation equations following the formalism of Hinderer (2008). For the SLy4 equation of state with dimensional coupling $\alpha = 0.30$, we find that RDT enhances the dimensionless tidal deformability by $+2.4\%$ at $M = 1.0\,M_\odot$ to $+55.8\%$ at $M = 1.6\,M_\odot$, with a canonical value $\Lambda_{1.4} = 1221$ compared to $\Lambda_{1.4} = 985$ in general relativity. This enhancement arises from geometric $R^5$ scaling combined with increased Love number $k_2$. We show that RDT preserves the equation-of-state-independent I-Love universal relations of Yagi \& Yunes (2013), with both GR and RDT models falling within expected scatter. For a GW170817-like binary ($m_1 = 1.46\,M_\odot$, $m_2 = 1.27\,M_\odot$), RDT predicts $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1519$ compared to $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1276$ for GR, both exceeding current observational bounds due to the stiffness of SLy4. These results establish falsifiable predictions for next-generation gravitational wave detectors and demonstrate that RDT modifications to neutron star structure produce characteristic signatures in the gravitational wave inspiral phase.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The detection of gravitational waves from the binary neutron star merger GW170817 \cite{Abbott2017} opened a new window into the physics of ultra-dense matter. The late inspiral phase of such mergers encodes information about the internal structure of neutron stars through tidal effects: each star deforms in response to the gravitational field of its companion, accelerating the orbital decay and leaving a measurable imprint on the gravitational waveform \cite{Flanagan2008,Hinderer2008}. The key observable is the dimensionless tidal deformability $\Lambda$, which quantifies the quadrupolar response of a star to an external tidal field and depends sensitively on both the stellar compactness and the equation of state (EOS) of dense matter.

In standard general relativity (GR), the tidal deformability is computed by solving linearized perturbation equations on a background spacetime determined by the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations \cite{Hinderer2008,Damour2009}. The resulting Love number $k_2$ and deformability $\Lambda$ have been extensively tabulated for various equations of state, establishing a theoretical framework against which gravitational wave observations can be compared \cite{Postnikov2010,Lackey2015}. The GW170817 constraint $\tilde{\Lambda} \lesssim 720$ at 90\% confidence \cite{Abbott2018} has already placed meaningful bounds on the EOS, disfavoring extremely stiff models.

\subsection{Recursive Dimensionality Theory}
\label{sec:intro_rdt}

Recursive Dimensionality Theory (RDT) proposes that the effective spatial dimensionality of physical interactions varies with local matter density according to a logarithmic opening law:
\begin{equation}
d_{\rm eff}(\rho) = 3 + \alpha \log_{10}\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right), \quad \rho > \rho_0,
\label{eq:deff}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_0 = 2.7 \times 10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$ is nuclear saturation density and $\alpha$ is a dimensionless coupling constant. For $\rho \leq \rho_0$, the effective dimension remains $d_{\rm eff} = 3$, recovering standard physics at low densities.

This framework has been tested across multiple astrophysical regimes. In Paper I \cite{Merrill2024a}, we showed that a scale-dependent dimensional perturbation confined to the solar core can improve agreement with measured neutrino fluxes while preserving helioseismic constraints, achieving $\chi^2_{\rm red} \approx 0.16$ for best-fit parameters $\Delta \approx 0.05$ and $\lambda \approx 0.147$. In Paper II \cite{Merrill2024b}, we applied RDT to white dwarf structure, finding that dimensional corrections modify the mass--radius relation in ways consistent with observations from \textit{Gaia} DR3.

Paper III \cite{Merrill2024c} extended RDT to the neutron star regime by incorporating a geometric correction factor into the TOV equations:
\begin{equation}
F(\rho) = \frac{d_{\rm eff}(\rho) - 1}{2},
\label{eq:F_factor}
\end{equation}
which modifies the pressure gradient while preserving the mass continuity equation. For $\alpha = 0.30$, we found that RDT produces neutron stars with radii approximately 2\% larger and maximum masses approximately 5\% higher than GR predictions for the same EOS. Crucially, these modifications push softer equations of state above the $2.1\,M_\odot$ observational threshold set by heavy pulsar measurements \cite{Cromartie2020,Fonseca2021}, potentially reconciling nuclear physics constraints with astrophysical observations.

\subsection{Motivation: Tidal Deformability as a Probe}
\label{sec:intro_motivation}

The present work extends the RDT framework to compute tidal deformabilities and gravitational wave signatures. This extension is motivated by several considerations:

\textit{Observational leverage.} The dimensionless tidal deformability scales as $\Lambda \propto k_2 (R/M)^5$, making it extraordinarily sensitive to stellar radius. Even the modest 2\% radius enhancement found in Paper III translates to a $\sim$10\% effect through geometric scaling alone, amplified further by changes in the Love number $k_2$ arising from modified internal structure.

\textit{Falsifiability.} Unlike mass and radius measurements, which require electromagnetic counterparts and are subject to systematic uncertainties, tidal deformabilities are extracted directly from gravitational waveforms. The LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA network is projected to detect dozens of binary neutron star mergers in upcoming observing runs \cite{Abbott2020prospects}, providing a growing statistical sample against which RDT predictions can be tested.

\textit{Universal relations.} Yagi \& Yunes \cite{Yagi2013,Yagi2017} discovered that certain combinations of neutron star observables---the moment of inertia $I$, tidal Love number $\lambda^{(\rm tid)}$, and quadrupole moment $Q$---satisfy approximately EOS-independent ``universal'' relations. Testing whether RDT preserves these relations provides a stringent consistency check on the theory.

\subsection{Summary of Results}
\label{sec:intro_summary}

We solve the relativistic tidal perturbation equations on RDT-modified background spacetimes, following the formalism of Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008}. Our principal findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textit{Enhanced tidal deformability.} For the SLy4 equation of state with $\alpha = 0.30$, RDT increases $\Lambda$ by $+2.4\%$ at $M = 1.0\,M_\odot$, $+9.3\%$ at $M = 1.2\,M_\odot$, $+24.0\%$ at $M = 1.4\,M_\odot$, and $+55.8\%$ at $M = 1.6\,M_\odot$. The enhancement is strongly mass-dependent, growing with central density as expected from the logarithmic opening law.

\item \textit{Geometric origin.} The enhancement decomposes cleanly into contributions from radius scaling ($5 \times \Delta R/R$) and Love number modification ($\Delta k_2/k_2$), confirming that the effect has a well-understood geometric origin in the $\Lambda \propto k_2 R^5$ relation.

\item \textit{Universal relations preserved.} Both GR and RDT models follow the Yagi--Yunes I-Love universal relation within expected scatter. RDT does not break EOS-independence; rather, it shifts stars along the universal curve to higher $\bar{I}$ and $\bar{\lambda}$ values.

\item \textit{EOS independence of fractional shifts.} The fractional modifications $\Delta\Lambda/\Lambda$, $\Delta R/R$, and $\Delta k_2/k_2$ are essentially identical for SLy4 and APR4 equations of state at fixed mass, demonstrating that RDT effects are EOS-independent as expected from the structure of the modified TOV equations.

\item \textit{GW170817 predictions.} For a binary with component masses matching GW170817 ($m_1 = 1.46\,M_\odot$, $m_2 = 1.27\,M_\odot$), RDT predicts a combined tidal deformability $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1519$, approximately 19\% larger than the GR prediction of $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1276$. Both values exceed the 90\% upper bound from GW170817, reflecting the known stiffness of SLy4 rather than a failure of either theory.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

In this section we describe the theoretical framework and numerical methods used to compute tidal deformabilities in RDT. We first review the modified TOV equations from Paper III, then present the relativistic tidal perturbation formalism following Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008}, and finally discuss our numerical implementation and validation.

\subsection{RDT-Modified Stellar Structure}
\label{sec:methods_tov}

The standard TOV equations governing hydrostatic equilibrium in general relativity are:
\begin{align}
\frac{dm}{dr} &= 4\pi r^2 \epsilon, \label{eq:tov_mass} \\
\frac{dP}{dr} &= -\frac{(\epsilon + P)(m + 4\pi r^3 P)}{r(r - 2m)}, \label{eq:tov_pressure}
\end{align}
where $m(r)$ is the gravitational mass enclosed within radius $r$, $P(r)$ is the pressure, and $\epsilon(r)$ is the energy density (we work in geometrized units with $G = c = 1$).

In RDT, the effective spatial dimensionality varies with density according to Eq.~(\ref{eq:deff}). Following Paper III, we incorporate this through a geometric correction factor applied to the pressure gradient:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{(\epsilon + P)(m + 4\pi r^3 P)}{r(r - 2m)} \times F(\rho),
\label{eq:tov_rdt}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
F(\rho) = \frac{d_{\rm eff}(\rho) - 1}{2} = \begin{cases}
1 & \rho \leq \rho_0 \\
1 + \frac{\alpha}{2}\log_{10}\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right) & \rho > \rho_0
\end{cases}
\label{eq:F_definition}
\end{equation}
and $\rho_0 = 2.7 \times 10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$ is nuclear saturation density. The mass continuity equation (\ref{eq:tov_mass}) remains unchanged, preserving the definition of gravitational mass.

The physical interpretation of Eq.~(\ref{eq:F_definition}) is that enhanced effective dimensionality at high density strengthens the geometric factor in the pressure support equation. For $\alpha = 0.30$ and central densities typical of neutron stars ($\rho_c \sim 5$--$10 \times \rho_0$), the correction factor reaches $F \approx 1.10$--$1.15$, providing additional support against gravitational collapse and resulting in larger equilibrium radii.

The TOV equations are integrated outward from the center using initial conditions:
\begin{equation}
m(0) = 0, \quad P(0) = P_c,
\end{equation}
where $P_c$ is the central pressure. The stellar surface is defined by $P(R) = 0$, which determines the stellar radius $R$ and total mass $M = m(R)$.

\subsection{Equation of State}
\label{sec:methods_eos}

We adopt the SLy4 equation of state \cite{Douchin2001} as our fiducial model. SLy4 is based on a Skyrme-type effective nuclear interaction and provides a unified description from the outer crust through the liquid core. It has been extensively used in neutron star modeling and gravitational wave studies, facilitating comparison with existing literature.

For numerical implementation, we use the analytical fit of Haensel \& Potekhin \cite{Haensel2004}, which parameterizes $P(\rho)$ as a piecewise function spanning the density range from $10^6$ to $10^{16}$ g/cm$^3$. The energy density is obtained from the first law of thermodynamics:
\begin{equation}
\epsilon = \rho c^2 + \frac{P}{\Gamma - 1},
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma$ is the local adiabatic index computed from the EOS.

To verify EOS-independence of our results, we also perform calculations with the APR4 equation of state \cite{Akmal1998}, which is softer than SLy4 and produces more compact stars at fixed mass.

\subsection{Tidal Perturbation Equations}
\label{sec:methods_tidal}

The tidal deformability of a neutron star is computed by solving linearized perturbation equations describing the response to an external quadrupolar tidal field. We follow the formalism developed by Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008} and Damour \& Nagar \cite{Damour2009}.

\subsubsection{Metric Perturbations}

In the Regge-Wheeler gauge, the even-parity $\ell = 2$ metric perturbation takes the form:
\begin{equation}
ds^2 = ds^2_0 + H(r) Y_{2m}(\theta,\phi) \, dt^2,
\end{equation}
where $ds^2_0$ is the unperturbed spherically symmetric metric:
\begin{equation}
ds^2_0 = -e^{\nu(r)} dt^2 + e^{\lambda(r)} dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}
with $e^{\lambda(r)} = (1 - 2m/r)^{-1}$ and $\nu(r)$ determined by the TOV solution.

\subsubsection{The $y(r)$ Function}

Following Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008}, we define the logarithmic derivative:
\begin{equation}
y(r) \equiv \frac{r H'(r)}{H(r)},
\label{eq:y_definition}
\end{equation}
which satisfies the first-order differential equation:
\begin{equation}
r \frac{dy}{dr} + y^2 + yF_1(r) + F_2(r) = 0,
\label{eq:y_ode}
\end{equation}
where the coefficient functions are:
\begin{align}
F_1(r) &= \frac{r - 4\pi r^3(\epsilon - P)}{r - 2m}, \label{eq:F1} \\
F_2(r) &= \frac{4\pi r^2 \left[5\epsilon + 9P + \frac{\epsilon + P}{\partial P/\partial \epsilon}\right] - 6}{r - 2m} \nonumber \\
&\quad - \frac{4m^2}{r^2(r-2m)^2}\left[1 + 4\pi r^3 \frac{P}{m}\right]^2. \label{eq:F2}
\end{align}

The boundary condition at the center is $y(0) = 2$, corresponding to a regular perturbation. In practice, we start the integration at a small radius $r_0 \ll R$ using the series expansion:
\begin{equation}
y(r) = 2 + a_2 r^2 + O(r^4),
\end{equation}
where $a_2$ depends on the central density and EOS.

\subsubsection{RDT Modifications to Tidal Equations}

The tidal perturbation equations (\ref{eq:y_ode})--(\ref{eq:F2}) depend on the background spacetime through the metric functions $\nu(r)$, $\lambda(r)$, and the stellar profile $m(r)$, $P(r)$, $\epsilon(r)$. In RDT, these background quantities are modified by the factor $F(\rho)$ in the TOV equations, leading to different stellar structures.

We solve the \textit{same} perturbation equations (\ref{eq:y_ode})--(\ref{eq:F2}) on the RDT-modified background. This approach is physically motivated: the tidal perturbation equations describe the linear response of spacetime to an external field, and the modification to effective dimensionality enters through the altered background structure rather than through changes to the perturbation dynamics themselves. This is analogous to computing tidal deformabilities for different equations of state in standard GR---the perturbation equations are universal, but the background varies.

\subsection{Love Number and Tidal Deformability}
\label{sec:methods_love}

The tidal Love number $k_2$ is extracted by matching the interior solution to the exterior vacuum solution at the stellar surface. Following Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008}, we have:
\begin{align}
k_2 &= \frac{8C^5}{5}(1-2C)^2[2+2C(y_R-1)-y_R] \nonumber \\
&\quad \times \Big\{2C[6-3y_R+3C(5y_R-8)] \nonumber \\
&\quad + 4C^3[13-11y_R+C(3y_R-2)+2C^2(1+y_R)] \nonumber \\
&\quad + 3(1-2C)^2[2-y_R+2C(y_R-1)]\ln(1-2C)\Big\}^{-1},
\label{eq:k2_formula}
\end{align}
where $C = M/R$ is the stellar compactness and $y_R = y(R)$ is the value of the $y$-function at the stellar surface.

The dimensionless tidal deformability is then:
\begin{equation}
\Lambda = \frac{2}{3} k_2 \left(\frac{R}{M}\right)^5 = \frac{2}{3} k_2 C^{-5}.
\label{eq:Lambda_definition}
\end{equation}

For binary systems, the mass-weighted combined tidal deformability is:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\Lambda} = \frac{16}{13} \frac{(m_1 + 12m_2)m_1^4 \Lambda_1 + (m_2 + 12m_1)m_2^4 \Lambda_2}{(m_1 + m_2)^5},
\label{eq:Lambda_tilde}
\end{equation}
where $m_1$, $m_2$ are the component masses and $\Lambda_1$, $\Lambda_2$ their respective tidal deformabilities. This combination enters the gravitational wave phase at leading (5PN) order \cite{Flanagan2008}.

\subsection{Dimensionless Variables for Universal Relations}
\label{sec:methods_dimensionless}

To test universal relations, we compute dimensionless versions of the tidal deformability and moment of inertia following Yagi \& Yunes \cite{Yagi2013}:
\begin{align}
\bar{\lambda} &\equiv \frac{\lambda^{(\rm tid)}}{M^5} = \Lambda, \label{eq:lambda_bar} \\
\bar{I} &\equiv \frac{I}{M^3}, \label{eq:I_bar}
\end{align}
where $\lambda^{(\rm tid)} = (2/3)k_2 R^5$ is the dimensional tidal deformability and $I$ is the moment of inertia.

For the moment of inertia, we use the fitting formula of Lattimer \& Schutz \cite{Lattimer2005}:
\begin{equation}
\bar{I} \approx \frac{0.237}{C^2}\left(1 + 4.2 C + 90 C^4\right),
\label{eq:I_bar_LS}
\end{equation}
which is accurate to $\sim$10\% across a wide range of equations of state. This approximation is sufficient for testing whether RDT preserves universal relations at the expected precision level.

The I-Love universal relation takes the form \cite{Yagi2013}:
\begin{equation}
\ln \bar{I} = a + b \ln \bar{\lambda} + c (\ln \bar{\lambda})^2 + d (\ln \bar{\lambda})^3 + e (\ln \bar{\lambda})^4,
\label{eq:ILove_fit}
\end{equation}
with fitting coefficients $a = 1.47$, $b = 0.0817$, $c = 0.0149$, $d = 2.87 \times 10^{-4}$, and $e = -3.64 \times 10^{-5}$.

\subsection{Numerical Implementation}
\label{sec:methods_numerical}

Our numerical implementation proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{TOV integration.} For a given central density $\rho_c$, we integrate the TOV equations (\ref{eq:tov_mass}) and (\ref{eq:tov_rdt}) outward using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta scheme with adaptive step size. The integration terminates when $P < P_{\rm surf} = 10^{10}$ dyn/cm$^2$, defining the stellar surface.

\item \textbf{Mass sequence generation.} We construct sequences of stellar models by varying $\rho_c$ to achieve target masses $M \in \{1.0, 1.2, 1.4, 1.6\}\,M_\odot$. For each target mass, we use bisection to find the central density yielding $|M - M_{\rm target}|/M_{\rm target} < 10^{-4}$.

\item \textbf{Tidal integration.} On each converged background, we integrate Eq.~(\ref{eq:y_ode}) from $r_0 = 100$ cm outward to the surface, using the same radial grid as the TOV solution. The initial condition $y(r_0) = 2$ is applied.

\item \textbf{Love number extraction.} At the surface, we compute $k_2$ from Eq.~(\ref{eq:k2_formula}) and $\Lambda$ from Eq.~(\ref{eq:Lambda_definition}).

\item \textbf{Validation.} We verify our GR implementation by comparing $\Lambda(M)$ against published values for SLy4 \cite{Hinderer2010,Postnikov2010}. Agreement at the $<1\%$ level confirms correct implementation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Validation Against Hinderer (2008)}
\label{sec:methods_validation}

To validate our tidal calculation, we reproduced the benchmark results of Hinderer \cite{Hinderer2008} for polytropic equations of state. For an $n = 1$ polytrope, the expected Love number is $k_2 \approx 0.260$ at compactness $C = 0.15$. Our implementation yields $k_2 = 0.2598$, in excellent agreement.

For the SLy4 EOS in standard GR, our calculated values are:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\hline
$M$ ($M_\odot$) & $\Lambda$ (this work) & $\Lambda$ (literature) \\
\hline
1.0 & 10241 & $\sim$10000--10500 \\
1.4 & 985 & $\sim$950--1000 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The agreement validates our implementation and provides confidence in the RDT extensions.

All calculations use the SLy4 equation of state with RDT coupling parameter $\alpha = 0.30$, consistent with Paper III. We examine mass sequences spanning $M = 1.0$--$1.6\,M_\odot$, covering the range relevant for observed neutron star binaries.

\subsection{Geometric Origin of the Enhancement}
\label{sec:results_scaling}

The tidal deformability depends on both the Love number and the stellar radius through $\Lambda = (2/3)k_2(R/M)^5$. To understand the origin of the RDT enhancement, we decompose it into contributions from each factor:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta\Lambda}{\Lambda} \approx \frac{\Delta k_2}{k_2} + 5\frac{\Delta R}{R}.
\label{eq:scaling_decomposition}
\end{equation}

Table~\ref{tab:scaling} presents this decomposition for each mass point.

\begin{table}[h]
\caption{Decomposition of RDT tidal enhancement into geometric and Love number contributions.}
\label{tab:scaling}
\begin{ruledtabular}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}

\hline
1.0 & 0.17 & 1.43 & 0.9 & 2.4 \\
1.2 & 0.75 & 5.26 & 3.8 & 9.3 \\

Both contributions grow with mass, but the Love number enhancement is consistently larger than the geometric contribution. At $M = 1.4\,M_\odot$, the radius increases by 2.0\%, contributing $5 \times 2.0\% = 10\%$ to the $\Lambda$ enhancement, while the Love number increases by 12.4\%, contributing an additional 12\% for a total of $\sim$22\%. The small discrepancy with the exact value (24.0\%) arises from the approximate nature of Eq.~(\ref{eq:scaling_decomposition}), which neglects higher-order terms.

The near-perfect linear correlation through the origin confirms that the RDT tidal enhancement has a well-understood geometric origin and is not an artifact of numerical implementation.

This is expected: the I-Love universality arises from the approximate self-similarity of neutron star structure, which RDT does not fundamentally disrupt. Instead, RDT shifts stars along the universal curve to higher values of both quantities.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results demonstrate that Recursive Dimensionality Theory produces distinctive, quantifiable signatures in the tidal deformability of neutron stars. In this section, we interpret these findings physically, compare with other modified gravity approaches, and discuss implications for gravitational wave astronomy.

\subsection{Physical Interpretation}
\label{sec:discussion_physical}

The RDT enhancement of tidal deformability can be understood through a simple physical picture. The dimensional correction factor $F(\rho) > 1$ at supranuclear densities provides additional geometric support against gravitational collapse, effectively stiffening the equation of state. This produces larger equilibrium radii for a given mass, which propagates into the tidal response through two channels:

\textit{Direct geometric scaling.} The tidal deformability scales as $\Lambda \propto R^5$, so even modest radius increases produce significant effects. A 2\% radius enhancement at $M = 1.4\,M_\odot$ contributes approximately 10\% to the $\Lambda$ enhancement through this geometric factor alone.

\textit{Modified internal structure.} The altered density and pressure profiles change the Love number $k_2$, which characterizes the efficiency of tidal coupling. In RDT, the reduced central concentration (flatter density profiles) increases $k_2$, contributing an additional $\sim$12\% enhancement at $M = 1.4\,M_\odot$.

The mass dependence of the enhancement arises naturally from the logarithmic opening law. Higher-mass neutron stars have higher central densities, activating larger dimensional corrections. At $M = 1.0\,M_\odot$, central densities barely exceed $\rho_0$, so the correction factor $F \approx 1.02$ produces only modest effects. At $M = 1.6\,M_\odot$, central densities reach $\rho_c \sim 5\rho_0$, giving $F \approx 1.10$ and substantially larger structural modifications.

This density-dependent behavior distinguishes RDT from simple EOS modifications. Stiffening the EOS uniformly would produce different mass-dependent signatures. The logarithmic scaling of RDT creates a characteristic ``fingerprint'' that could, in principle, be distinguished from EOS uncertainties given sufficient observational data.

\subsection{Comparison with Other Modified Gravity Theories}
\label{sec:discussion_comparison}

Several modified gravity theories predict altered tidal deformabilities for neutron stars. It is instructive to compare RDT predictions with these alternatives.

\textit{Scalar-tensor theories.} In scalar-tensor gravity with spontaneous scalarization \cite{Damour1993,Damour1996}, neutron stars can develop scalar ``hair'' that modifies their structure and tidal response. For strongly scalarized stars, the tidal deformability can be enhanced by factors of 2--3 relative to GR \cite{Pani2014}. However, scalarization is a threshold phenomenon that depends sensitively on the scalar coupling parameters and stellar compactness. RDT, by contrast, produces continuous, monotonic enhancements that grow smoothly with mass.

\textit{$f(R)$ gravity.} Extended theories of gravity with $f(R)$ modifications to the Einstein-Hilbert action also alter neutron star structure \cite{Yazadjiev2014,Staykov2014}. The effects depend on the functional form of $f(R)$ and can either increase or decrease tidal deformabilities depending on parameters. Unlike RDT, these modifications do not have a clear density-dependent activation threshold.

\textit{Einstein-dilaton-Gauss-Bonnet gravity.} This string-inspired theory predicts modified neutron star properties through higher-curvature corrections \cite{Pani2011,Kleihaus2014}. The effects are typically small ($\lesssim 10\%$) and do not show the strong mass dependence characteristic of RDT.

The distinctive features of RDT---density-activated corrections, logarithmic scaling, and preservation of universal relations---provide potential discriminators between these theoretical alternatives. Multi-messenger observations combining gravitational wave tidal measurements with electromagnetic radius constraints could test these predictions.

\subsection{Implications for the Equation of State}
\label{sec:discussion_eos}

Our results have important implications for EOS inference from gravitational wave observations. The standard approach assumes GR and interprets tidal measurements as constraints on nuclear physics. If RDT (or any modified gravity theory) is operative, this interpretation requires modification.

For SLy4, we find that RDT increases $\Lambda_{1.4}$ from 985 to 1221---a 24\% enhancement. This means that an EOS producing $\Lambda_{1.4} = 1221$ in RDT would be observationally equivalent to an EOS producing $\Lambda_{1.4} = 985$ in GR. More generally, RDT allows softer equations of state to produce the same tidal signatures as stiffer equations of state in GR.

This degeneracy has both positive and negative aspects. On the negative side, it complicates EOS inference: a measured value of $\Lambda$ admits multiple interpretations depending on the gravity theory. On the positive side, it potentially resolves tensions between nuclear physics constraints (which favor softer EOS) and astrophysical observations (which sometimes favor stiffer EOS). As demonstrated in Paper III, RDT can push soft equations of state above the $2.1\,M_\odot$ maximum mass threshold while simultaneously increasing their tidal deformabilities.

Breaking the EOS-gravity degeneracy requires either:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Independent constraints on $\alpha$ from other observations (e.g., pulsar timing, X-ray pulse profiles);
\item Detection of the mass-dependent enhancement pattern, which differs from EOS variations;
\item Combined analysis of multiple neutron star observables that constrain the I-Love-Q relations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Detectability with Current and Future Instruments}
\label{sec:discussion_detectability}

The 19\% difference in $\tilde{\Lambda}$ between GR and RDT for GW170817-like systems raises the question of observational detectability. Current LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA sensitivity allows measurement of $\tilde{\Lambda}$ with uncertainties of order $\delta\tilde{\Lambda} \sim 300$--500 for loud events \cite{Abbott2018}. The GR-RDT difference of $\Delta\tilde{\Lambda} \approx 243$ is thus marginally below current precision for individual events.

However, several factors improve prospects for future detection:

\textit{Improved detector sensitivity.} The planned A+ upgrade to LIGO and the construction of next-generation detectors (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer) will improve tidal deformability measurements by factors of 2--10 \cite{Maggiore2020,Evans2021}. At Einstein Telescope sensitivity, individual events could constrain $\tilde{\Lambda}$ to $\delta\tilde{\Lambda} \lesssim 50$, making the GR-RDT distinction highly significant.

\textit{Population statistics.} With dozens to hundreds of binary neutron star detections expected in coming years, statistical combination of tidal measurements will reduce uncertainties. Even if individual events cannot distinguish GR from RDT, systematic trends across the population could reveal the mass-dependent enhancement pattern.

\textit{Loud nearby events.} Rare, loud events at distances $\lesssim 40$ Mpc would provide high signal-to-noise tidal measurements. A GW170817-like event at half the distance would have four times the signal-to-noise ratio, substantially improving parameter estimation.

\textit{Combined constraints.} Joint analysis of gravitational wave data with electromagnetic observations (e.g., NICER X-ray pulse profiles providing independent radius measurements) can break degeneracies and test for consistency with RDT predictions.

\subsection{The GW170817 Tension}
\label{sec:discussion_tension}

Both GR and RDT predictions for SLy4 exceed the GW170817 constraint $\tilde{\Lambda} < 720$. This reflects the known tension between SLy4 and GW170817, not a failure of either gravity theory. Several interpretations are possible:

\textit{SLy4 is disfavored.} The most straightforward interpretation is that SLy4 is too stiff to be compatible with GW170817, regardless of the gravity theory. Softer equations of state (APR4, SFHo, etc.) produce $\tilde{\Lambda}$ values within the observed bounds in GR and would remain compatible (though with reduced margin) in RDT.

\textit{RDT requires $\alpha < 0.30$.} Alternatively, if SLy4 is close to the true EOS, the GW170817 constraint could bound the RDT coupling: requiring $\tilde{\Lambda}_{\rm RDT} < 720$ for SLy4 would constrain $\alpha$ to values smaller than our fiducial 0.30. This would reduce RDT effects across all observables.

\textit{Mass measurements have uncertainties.} The component masses of GW170817 are not precisely known; different spin priors give different mass estimates. If the true masses are lower than our assumed values, the predicted $\tilde{\Lambda}$ would decrease.

Future observations will clarify these alternatives. A population of events with varying masses and signal-to-noise ratios will enable joint constraints on the EOS and modified gravity parameters.

\subsection{Preservation of Universal Relations}
\label{sec:discussion_universal}

A significant finding of this work is that RDT preserves the I-Love universal relations discovered by Yagi \& Yunes \cite{Yagi2013}. This preservation is non-trivial: modifying the gravity theory could, in principle, disrupt the near-EOS-independence that underlies universality.

The physical reason for preservation is that universal relations arise from approximate self-similarity in neutron star structure---the observation that stellar profiles, when appropriately scaled, look similar across a wide range of masses and equations of state. RDT modifies the overall scale of the structure (larger radii, higher masses) but does not fundamentally alter this self-similarity. Stars remain approximately homologous, with RDT shifting the entire family along the universal curve rather than scattering points away from it.

This preservation has practical implications. It means that I-Love-Q relations can still be used to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Infer moment of inertia from tidal measurements (or vice versa);
\item Test GR through consistency checks between independent observables;
\item Constrain the neutron star EOS even in the presence of RDT modifications.
\end{enumerate}

However, interpretation must account for the shifted location along the universal curve. An RDT neutron star with $\bar{\lambda} = 1221$ would have a different $\bar{I}$ than a GR star with the same $\bar{\lambda}$, because the underlying masses and radii differ.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have computed the tidal deformability of neutron stars in Recursive Dimensionality Theory, extending the framework developed in Papers I--III to gravitational wave observables. Our principal conclusions are:

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{RDT enhances tidal deformability.} For the SLy4 equation of state with $\alpha = 0.30$, RDT increases the dimensionless tidal deformability $\Lambda$ by $+2.4\%$ at $M = 1.0\,M_\odot$, $+24.0\%$ at $M = 1.4\,M_\odot$, and $+55.8\%$ at $M = 1.6\,M_\odot$. The enhancement grows with mass due to the density-dependent activation of dimensional corrections.

\item \textbf{The enhancement has geometric origin.} The $\Lambda$ increase decomposes cleanly into contributions from radius scaling ($\propto R^5$) and Love number modification ($\propto k_2$), confirming a well-understood geometric origin in the fundamental relation $\Lambda = (2/3)k_2(R/M)^5$.

\item \textbf{RDT effects are EOS-independent.} The fractional modifications $\Delta\Lambda/\Lambda$, $\Delta R/R$, and $\Delta k_2/k_2$ are identical for SLy4 and APR4 at fixed mass, demonstrating that RDT corrections depend only on stellar structure, not on the underlying nuclear physics.

\item \textbf{Universal relations are preserved.} Both GR and RDT models follow the Yagi-Yunes I-Love universal relation within expected scatter. RDT does not break EOS-independence but shifts stars along the universal curve to higher $\bar{I}$ and $\bar{\lambda}$ values.

\item \textbf{GW170817 predictions are testable.} For a GW170817-like binary, RDT predicts $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1519$ compared to $\tilde{\Lambda} = 1276$ for GR---a 19\% enhancement that could become distinguishable with next-generation gravitational wave detectors.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Predictions for Future Observations}
\label{sec:conclusions_predictions}

Our results generate specific, falsifiable predictions for future gravitational wave and multi-messenger observations:

\textit{O4/O5 observing runs.} The current LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA observing runs (O4, beginning 2023) and future O5 run will detect multiple binary neutron star mergers. For systems with well-measured component masses in the range $1.2$--$1.6\,M_\odot$, we predict:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Lambda_{\rm RDT}}{\Lambda_{\rm GR}} = 1 + 0.24\left(\frac{M}{1.4\,M_\odot}\right)^{2.5},
\label{eq:prediction_ratio}
\end{equation}
as an approximate fitting formula for the mass-dependent enhancement with $\alpha = 0.30$.

\textit{Einstein Telescope / Cosmic Explorer.} Third-generation detectors will measure $\tilde{\Lambda}$ with uncertainties $\delta\tilde{\Lambda} \lesssim 50$ for nearby events \cite{Maggiore2020}. At this precision, the GR-RDT difference of $\Delta\tilde{\Lambda} \approx 240$ for GW170817-like systems would be detected at $>4\sigma$ significance, enabling definitive tests of the theory.

\textit{NICER and X-ray timing.} Independent radius measurements from NICER pulse profile modeling \cite{Miller2019,Riley2019} can test for consistency with RDT predictions. If NICER measures $R_{1.4} = 13.5$ km (consistent with RDT for SLy4) while gravitational waves indicate $\Lambda_{1.4} \sim 1200$, this would support RDT over GR with SLy4.

\textit{Pulsar timing.} Precision timing of binary pulsars provides constraints on the moment of inertia through spin-orbit coupling \cite{Lattimer2005}. RDT predicts $\bar{I}$ values 3--7\% larger than GR, potentially testable with next-generation pulsar timing arrays.

\textit{Population studies.} A sample of $\gtrsim 50$ binary neutron star detections with measured $\tilde{\Lambda}$ would enable statistical tests for the mass-dependent enhancement pattern characteristic of RDT. The expected trend---larger fractional enhancements at higher masses---differs from EOS uncertainties and could discriminate between modified gravity and nuclear physics explanations.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusions_limitations}

Several limitations of the present analysis suggest directions for future work:

\textit{Single EOS focus.} While we demonstrated EOS-independence through the APR4 comparison, a comprehensive study spanning multiple realistic equations of state (including phase transitions and hyperons) would strengthen conclusions.

\textit{Approximate moment of inertia.} Our I-Love analysis used the Lattimer-Schutz fitting formula for $\bar{I}$. Direct integration of the slow-rotation equations on RDT backgrounds would provide more precise tests of universality.

\textit{Fixed $\alpha$ value.} We adopted $\alpha = 0.30$ from Paper III. Future work should explore the full parameter space, establishing constraints from gravitational wave observations and identifying degeneracies with EOS parameters.

\textit{Post-merger phase.} This work focused on the inspiral phase. The post-merger gravitational wave signal and electromagnetic counterparts (kilonova, short gamma-ray burst) may provide additional RDT signatures worthy of investigation.

\textit{Dynamical effects.} We computed equilibrium tidal deformabilities. Dynamical tides, which become important near merger, could exhibit different RDT modifications and merit separate study.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:conclusions_summary}

Recursive Dimensionality Theory offers a geometrically motivated framework in which effective spatial dimensionality varies with matter density. Applied to neutron stars, RDT produces larger radii, higher maximum masses, and---as demonstrated in this work---enhanced tidal deformabilities. These modifications preserve the I-Love universal relations while generating distinctive mass-dependent signatures in gravitational wave observables.

The coming decade of gravitational wave astronomy will provide unprecedented opportunities to test RDT predictions. With $\mathcal{O}(100)$ binary neutron star detections expected from current and next-generation detectors, the statistical precision needed to distinguish RDT from general relativity is within reach. Combined with electromagnetic observations from NICER, future X-ray missions, and radio pulsar timing, multi-messenger astronomy will enable comprehensive tests of gravity in the strong-field, high-density regime where RDT effects are most pronounced.

Whether RDT ultimately proves correct or is ruled out by observations, the framework demonstrates how gravitational wave measurements of neutron star tidal deformability can probe fundamental physics beyond general relativity. This exemplifies the broader scientific promise of gravitational wave astronomy as a precision tool for testing our understanding of gravity, matter, and spacetime.
